How Wetlands Could Protect Against Hurricanes and Floods

Grassy wetlands act as a buffer against storm surges, giving hurricanes more to conquer before reaching people and buildings. Much of New Yorkâ€™s waterfronts are wetlands, but they mostly exist around Staten Island, Long Island and Jamaica Bay.
Wetlands act like sponges and can hold onto water from storms. The water is stored and later released at a slower rate than it would be during a hurricane.
The reduction in water flow helps debris and other solids settle into the land before being released onto land or into other bodies of water. Biofilm, a cluster of bacteria on the surface of wetlands, collect some pollutants. This process helps store debris from high winds.
The plants and low-lying areas of wetlands weaken the overall energy of winds. The ecosystem of wetlands can also lessen the wave heights of storm surges.
